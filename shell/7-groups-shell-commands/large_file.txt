PAGE 1
======
The terminal is one of humanity’s quieter inventions.
No buttons. No icons. No comforting progress bars.
Just text, waiting patiently for you to say something intelligent.

Most people meet the terminal by accident.
A tutorial told them to.
A colleague said, “It’s faster.”
A bug refused to die unless confronted at the command line.

At first, it feels hostile.
Why does it shout?
Why does it fail silently?
Why does one missing space ruin everything?

But slowly, patterns emerge.
Commands become sentences.
Flags become adjectives.
Pipelines become thoughts flowing from one idea to the next.

--------------------------------------------------

PAGE 2
======
Before graphical interfaces, there was only intention.
You didn’t click.
You described.

“List the files.”
“Move this there.”
“Show me only the lines that matter.”

The shell does not guess what you want.
It executes exactly what you say.
This makes it brutally honest—and incredibly powerful.

Mistakes are not hidden.
They are displayed, often immediately.
And that, paradoxically, makes learning faster.

--------------------------------------------------

PAGE 3
======
Files are the atoms of computing.
Small files form scripts.
Scripts form tools.
Tools form workflows.

A data analyst eventually notices something:
Most problems are not about algorithms.
They are about *finding, filtering, and reshaping text*.

Logs.
CSV files.
JSON blobs.
System outputs.

The shell thrives in this chaos.

--------------------------------------------------

PAGE 4
======
The `less` command exists because humans read differently from machines.
Machines ingest entire files instantly.
Humans skim.
Pause.
Search.
Scroll backward.
Reread.

`less` respects that.
It does not rush.
It waits for your curiosity.

Forward.
Backward.
Search.
Quit.

Nothing more.
Nothing less.

--------------------------------------------------

PAGE 5
======
Try pressing:
- `Space` to move forward
- `b` to move backward
- `/data` to search for a word
- `n` to repeat a search
- `q` to quit

Notice how your hands start to remember.
This is muscle memory forming.
A quiet upgrade.

--------------------------------------------------

PAGE 6
======
Power tools often look unimpressive.
A hammer is just metal and wood.
A chisel is just sharp steel.
A shell command is just text.

But mastery hides in simplicity.

People who fear the terminal often fear precision.
The terminal demands clarity.
Ambiguity is punished.
Accuracy is rewarded.

--------------------------------------------------

PAGE 7
======
Eventually, the terminal stops feeling like a tool.
It becomes a place.

A workspace.
A lab bench.
A conversation partner that never interrupts
and never forgets what you typed five seconds ago.

History matters here.
Literally.
There is a `history` command.

--------------------------------------------------

PAGE 8
======
You will forget commands.
Everyone does.
That’s fine.

What matters is understanding *what kind of problem* you are solving:
- Navigating
- Inspecting
- Transforming
- Automating

The shell is less about memorization
and more about composition.

--------------------------------------------------

PAGE 9
======
If graphical tools are like driving automatic cars,
the shell is learning to drive manual.

Harder at first.
More stalls.
More noise.

But once learned,
you feel the machine responding directly to your intent.
